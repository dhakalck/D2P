D2P Implementation in Thailand

The Data to Policy program was launched in Thailand in 2018 in partnership with the Ministry of Public Health (MoPH) and the Department of Disease Control. The program has successfully built capacity among mid-level health officials to translate data into actionable policy recommendations.

Motorcycle Safety: From Data to Enforcement

Policy Brief: Strengthening Motorcycle Helmet Enforcement | Year: 2018

Thailand has one of the highest rates of road traffic deaths globally, with motorcyclists accounting for over 70% of fatalities. Despite helmet laws, enforcement was weak and compliance varied dramatically by region.

A D2P team from the Department of Disease Control conducted a comprehensive analysis comparing helmet use rates, enforcement strategies, and injury outcomes across provinces. Their economic evaluation showed that enhanced enforcement through police checkpoints and automated camera systems would be highly cost-effective, preventing an estimated 400 deaths and 2,000 serious injuries annually.

The Impact: The policy brief was presented to the Road Safety Committee and provincial governors. Within 6 months, enforcement budgets were increased in 15 high-risk provinces, and automated helmet detection cameras were installed on major highways. Compliance rates increased from 43% to 67% in targeted areas within one year.

"The D2P training gave us the tools to move beyond anecdotal evidence and build an economic case that decision-makers couldn't ignore. We showed that every baht invested in enforcement would save 15 baht in medical costs and lost productivity."

‚Äî Dr. Somchai Phanichkul, Team Leader, Department of Disease Control

Tobacco Control: Expanding Health Warning Labels

Policy Brief: Graphic Health Warning Labels | Year: 2019

Thailand was an early adopter of graphic health warnings on cigarette packages, but the 55% coverage requirement had remained unchanged since 2005. A D2P team investigated whether expanding warnings to 85% coverage would be effective and cost-effective.

The team's analysis drew on international evidence and local smoking cessation data to model the impact of larger warnings. Their economic evaluation projected that expanded warnings would lead to 180,000 additional quit attempts annually, preventing 12,000 premature deaths over 10 years at minimal cost.

The Impact: The policy brief was presented at a national tobacco control conference and to the Tobacco Control Board. It provided crucial economic evidence supporting a policy change that was already under consideration. In October 2019, Thailand implemented new regulations requiring health warnings to cover 85% of cigarette packages‚Äîone of the largest in the world.

"D2P taught us how to frame our recommendations in economic terms that resonated with policymakers. We weren't just saying 'bigger warnings are better'‚Äîwe quantified exactly how many lives would be saved and showed the policy would pay for itself."

‚Äî Dr. Pimchanok Sriworaporn, Bureau of Non-Communicable Diseases
